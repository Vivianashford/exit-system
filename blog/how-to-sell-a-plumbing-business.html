How to Sell a Plumbing Business: A Practical Walkthrough for Owners Who Built Something Real

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
March 15, 2026 • 8 min read

In the trades, the most common scenario looks like this: a plumbing contractor doing $8-12M in revenue, approaching retirement, gets a broker valuation that feels low. The reason is almost always the same - owner dependency, inconsistent financials, or a revenue mix that is installation-heavy with no recurring service base. According to IBBA transaction data, the spread between what trade business owners expect and what they actually receive at close is frequently 30-50%.

That gap is not fixed by finding a better buyer. It is fixed by doing the preparation work that most owners skip. Knowing how to sell a plumbing business properly - with the right preparation, the right buyer type, and the right structure - is what separates a generational wealth event from a disappointing close.

What a Plumbing Business Is Actually Worth

The core valuation method buyers use for plumbing businesses is a multiple of EBITDA - Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. For a straightforward residential plumbing shop in the $3M to $8M revenue range, you're typically looking at 3.0x to 4.5x EBITDA from a financial buyer. For a business with recurring maintenance contracts, commercial relationships, and a strong operations manager in place, strategic buyers and private equity-backed platforms have paid 5.0x to 6.5x.

That spread matters. A $9M revenue plumbing company with $1.1M in adjusted EBITDA could sell for $3.3M at 3.0x or $7.2M at 6.5x. The business itself is the same. What changed is the buyer type and how well the owner prepared.

The variables that push a multiple up:

  • Recurring service contract revenue (20%+ of total revenue is meaningful)
  • A general manager or operations lead who doesn't need the owner in the building
  • Clean, consistent financials for at least three years
  • Documented processes and a trained field team
  • Geographic concentration that makes integration easy for an acquirer

The variables that compress a multiple - sometimes dramatically:

  • Owner-run operations where the owner is also the lead estimator, the relationship holder, and the problem-solver
  • Customer concentration (one commercial client representing 30%+ of revenue)
  • Aging equipment that needs immediate replacement capital
  • Revenue booked on a cash basis or inconsistently categorized in the books

Who Actually Buys Plumbing Businesses

Understanding buyer types changes your entire approach to how to sell a plumbing business. There are four real categories to know.

Individual buyers are entrepreneurs looking for an operator-opportunity. They want to step into your role. They will typically pay in the 2.5x to 3.5x EBITDA range and rely heavily on SBA financing. The deal process is slower and more personal. They care a lot about the owner's involvement in transition.

Strategic buyers are established plumbing or home services businesses looking to expand into your market, buy your customer list, or acquire your licensed workforce. They often pay more because they can realize synergies you can't value yourself. A larger regional plumbing company might pay 4.5x to 5.5x for a business that fills a geographic gap.

Private equity-backed platforms are the most active buyers in the trades right now. These are PE firms that have made one acquisition in a trade vertical and are now rolling up competitors. They move faster, often paying 5x to 7x for the right business, but they want clean books, transferable systems, and leadership that stays post-close. They're not buying your labor - they're buying your infrastructure.

Family offices and search funds occupy the middle ground. They're disciplined acquirers who often pay fair multiples and move at a reasonable pace. Less common in the trades but worth knowing.

The Preparation That Actually Changes the Outcome

Most owners who ask how to sell a plumbing business are thinking about the transaction itself. The listing, the broker, the offer. That's the last 20% of the process. The first 80% is preparation - and it takes 12 to 24 months done right.

The highest-value moves during the preparation phase:

Clean up your financials for three full years. Run your P&L like a buyer is reviewing it tomorrow. Separate personal expenses. Make add-backs clear and defensible. Inconsistent accounting is one of the fastest ways to kill a deal in due diligence or force a price reduction.

Build your EBITDA number deliberately. Adjusted EBITDA - the number buyers actually pay on - includes add-backs for owner salary above market rate, one-time expenses, and non-recurring costs. Document every legitimate add-back. Work with an accountant who understands M&A, not just tax filing.

Reduce owner dependency. This is covered in more depth in our article on owner-dependent business valuation, but the core principle is simple: if the business needs you in the room to run, it's worth less than one that doesn't.

Lock in your top people. Buyers of plumbing businesses worry about key technician retention. If your two best plumbers might walk when you do, that's a risk that gets priced in. Employment agreements and retention bonuses tied to post-close tenure address this directly.

The owners who exit at 5x to 6x EBITDA didn't find a better buyer. They built a better business to sell.

How the Deal Process Actually Works

Once preparation is complete and you're ready to go to market, the typical process for selling a plumbing business runs through these phases:

Months 1-2: Positioning and marketing materials. A quality advisor prepares a Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) - a detailed document presenting your business to prospective buyers. This is your business on paper. It should tell a compelling story about growth, systems, and opportunity.

Months 2-4: Buyer outreach and IOIs. Your advisor approaches qualified buyers under NDA. Interested parties submit Indications of Interest - initial, non-binding offers. This is where you see what the market thinks your business is worth.

Month 4-5: Management presentations and LOI. Serious buyers meet with you. After that, you receive a Letter of Intent from your preferred buyer. The LOI includes the purchase price, deal structure, and key terms. Most LOIs include an exclusivity period of 30 to 90 days.

Months 5-8: Due diligence. The buyer's team goes deep. Financials, operations, contracts, employees, equipment, licenses. A well-prepared seller moves through due diligence in 60 days. A poorly prepared seller watches deals fall apart here.

Month 8+: Close and transition. Final purchase agreement, legal review, wire transfer. Most deals include a transition period where the seller stays on for 3 to 12 months.

Deal Structure: What You Take Home Matters as Much as the Price

A $5M offer isn't always better than a $4M offer. Structure matters. In a typical plumbing business acquisition, you'll encounter some combination of:

  • Cash at close - the safest component; target 70%+ of purchase price in this category
  • Seller note - you finance part of the deal; you get paid over time with interest, but you take repayment risk
  • Earnout - additional payments tied to post-close performance; high risk if you're no longer running the business
  • Equity rollover - you keep a stake in the combined entity; can be very valuable in a PE platform rollup if they do a subsequent larger exit

Be skeptical of heavy earnout structures. If a buyer needs you to stay for three years to hit a number, they're not paying for what you built - they're paying for what you'll build for them.

What This Looks Like in Practice

The pattern we consistently see with well-prepared plumbing businesses: owners who spend 12-18 months building a service agreement program, installing an operations manager, and cleaning up their books typically see their adjusted EBITDA grow 20-35% during the preparation period - not from working harder, but from tighter financial management and recurring revenue that was already there to be captured. When they go to market with that track record, PE-backed platforms and strategic acquirers compete for the deal.

Industry data from BizBuySell shows that prepared sellers achieve transaction multiples 1.0x to 1.5x higher than the median for comparable businesses. On a $1.2M EBITDA business, that is $1.2M to $1.8M in additional proceeds. That is the scale of what preparation is worth. Not better luck. Not a better market. Better execution.
