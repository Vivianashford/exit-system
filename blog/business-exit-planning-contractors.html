Business Exit Planning for Contractors: Start Three Years Before You Think You Need To

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
March 10, 2026 • 9 min read

Industry data from BizBuySell shows that roughly 80% of businesses listed for sale never actually close. In the trades, the gap between what owners expect and what buyers offer is one of the most consistent patterns. A mechanical contractor doing $7M in revenue who hasn't addressed owner dependency, hasn't documented recurring revenue, and hasn't cleaned up financials for three consecutive years - that contractor will get an offer that feels low. And it won't be unfair. It will be an accurate price for an unprepared business.

The difference between a $3.8M offer and a $5.5M close on the same business is almost always preparation time. Not market timing. Not a better broker. Preparation.

Why Business Exit Planning for Contractors Takes Longer Than You Think

The construction and trades industry has a specific problem that most generalist business brokers don't fully understand: contractor businesses are deeply founder-dependent, and that dependency takes time to unwind.

A buyer doing due diligence on a $6M contracting business will ask one central question: does this business work without this person? If the answer is no, or if the answer requires a lot of qualification, the buyer either walks or reprices the deal to reflect the risk.

Removing that dependency isn't a task you complete in 90 days. It requires installing leadership, documenting operational systems, transferring customer relationships, and giving those changes enough runway to show up in the financial results. That process takes 18 to 36 months for most contractor businesses.

Here's a rough framework for how business exit planning for contractors should be sequenced:

Year One: Foundations and Financial Clarity

The first 12 months of preparation focus on two things: cleaning up what buyers will evaluate and identifying the gaps that are costing you multiple points.

Get a quality-of-earnings analysis. Not a valuation from a business broker trying to list you. An actual QofE from a firm that does M&A financial diligence. This tells you what a buyer's accountant will find before the buyer's accountant finds it. Surprises in due diligence kill deals or force price reductions. Find them yourself first.

Standardize your financial reporting. If your bookkeeper is using QuickBooks inconsistently, or your P&L looks different each year because of how you categorize owner draws and equipment purchases, fix it. Three years of clean, consistent financials are the foundation of any credible sale process.

Identify your EBITDA add-backs. The adjusted EBITDA number is what buyers pay on. Add-backs - owner compensation above market rate, personal expenses run through the business, one-time legal fees, non-recurring equipment costs - can add $100,000 to $400,000 to your effective EBITDA. Document every one of them with supporting evidence before you go to market.

Map your customer concentration. If any single customer represents more than 15% of your revenue, that's a risk that needs to be addressed. Not necessarily eliminated - but managed, documented, and explained in a way that gives a buyer confidence.

Year Two: Operations and Leadership Transfer

The second phase of business exit planning for contractors is operational. This is where the work gets harder - and where most owners either do it or don't.

Install or develop your operations leader. The single most important thing you can do to increase your valuation is have a general manager or operations director who can run the business when you're not there. Not because they're perfect - because they're proof the business doesn't depend on you. Buyers will test this. They'll ask to meet this person. They'll look at how the business has run when you've been away.

Document your processes. Estimating, job management, field crew protocols, subcontractor relationships, vendor terms, customer onboarding. This doesn't need to be a 400-page operations manual. It needs to be enough that someone new could understand how the business works without asking you every question. A systematic business is worth more than an intuitive one.

Convert project work to recurring relationships where possible. Service agreements, maintenance contracts, preferred vendor relationships - anything that creates predictable, repeating revenue. Buyers value recurring revenue at a meaningfully higher multiple than project revenue. Even a modest shift from 10% to 25% recurring can move your multiple by 0.5x to 1.0x.

The business you build to run without you is worth two to three times more than the business you built to run through you.

The 12 Months Before You Go to Market

With the foundation in place, the pre-market phase focuses on positioning and process.

Select your advisor carefully. Most small business brokers work on commission and volume. They list everything, work few deals hard, and accept whatever the market offers. For a contractor business above $5M in revenue, you need an M&A advisor who works with trade and service businesses, understands PE buyer dynamics, and can run a structured process that creates competitive tension among buyers.

Know your number and your terms. Before you receive a single offer, decide what a successful exit looks like to you. What's your minimum acceptable cash at close? How much transition period are you willing to commit to? Are you open to rolling equity in a PE platform? Having clear answers to these questions before you're in a negotiation keeps you from making bad decisions under pressure.

Time the market, but don't overthink it. Trade businesses are transacting at strong multiples right now. PE platforms continue to consolidate the trades vertical aggressively, and interest rates haven't materially slowed their activity because they're strategic buyers, not purely financial ones. If your business is ready, the market is ready.

The Most Common Exit Planning Mistakes Contractors Make

After working through exits in the trades for years, these are the mistakes that show up most often:

  • Waiting until they're burned out. A founder who is clearly disengaged sends a signal to buyers. They wonder if the business is already declining, and they price accordingly.
  • Using a generalist broker for a specialized business. Your electrical or mechanical contracting business is not the same as a sandwich shop. The buyer universe is different. The diligence questions are different. The deal structures are different.
  • Treating the sale as a secret. Key employees who find out about a sale from a due diligence process - rather than from you - feel deceived. Managing the communication around a potential sale is a legitimate leadership challenge, and advisors who have done it before know how to handle it.
  • Underestimating the tax impact. How your entity is structured, whether you do an asset sale or stock sale, how you handle the allocation of purchase price - these decisions can move your after-tax proceeds by hundreds of thousands of dollars. Get a CPA with M&A transaction experience involved before you sign an LOI.

What Good Exit Planning Actually Delivers

Done right, business exit planning for contractors is not about squeezing every dollar out of a sale. It's about building a business that earns a premium because it genuinely deserves one. A well-run, well-documented, leadership-enabled contracting business is rare. Buyers know it. And they pay for it.

The exit planning work described in this article is what separates a 3.5x transaction from a 5.5x transaction. Not better negotiation. Not a luckier market. A business built so that a buyer can own it with confidence. That's what exit planning is for.
