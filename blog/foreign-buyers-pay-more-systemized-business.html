Why Foreign Buyers Pay More for Systemized Service Businesses

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
April 1, 2026 • 10 min read Updated: April 2026

You have spent 25 or 30 years building something. A real business. One that employs people, serves a community, and generates serious cash flow year after year. And when the time comes to exit, you expect to sit across the table from a private equity firm that will run their numbers, offer you 4x or 5x EBITDA, and call it a day.

That is not the only table available to you. And it is almost certainly not the best one.

There is a category of buyer that most business owners in America have never seriously considered - and that most brokers will never think to approach. These are foreign strategic acquirers. Specifically, large international conglomerates - many of them Japanese - that have been quietly buying well-run, systemized US service businesses at premiums that domestic private equity simply cannot match. Not because they are irrational. Because they are playing a different game entirely.

If you want to understand why foreign buyers pay more - and how to position your business to sell your service business to a foreign buyer at a premium - you need to understand the strategic logic driving this trend. Because once you see it, you will never look at your exit the same way again.

Japanese homebuilders now control approximately 6% of US residential homebuilding through strategic acquisitions - paying 1-2x EBITDA premiums over comparable domestic PE bids.

The Japanese Homebuilder Playbook - and What It Means for You

Start with one of the most striking acquisition trends in American business over the past decade. Japanese homebuilders - Sekisui House, Daiwa House, Sumitomo Forestry, Iida Group - have systematically acquired US residential homebuilding operations. Not dabbled. Systematically acquired. Today, Japanese-owned companies account for roughly 6% of all new homes built in the United States.

Think about that number. Six percent of US homebuilding - one of the largest and most fragmented sectors in the American economy - is now controlled by companies headquartered in Tokyo and Osaka. This did not happen by accident. It happened because Japanese conglomerates identified something that domestic buyers were undervaluing, and they paid accordingly.

What were they buying? Not just revenue. Not just EBITDA. They were buying transferable operational systems. The ability to take a business built on American soil and run it from 6,000 miles away - or to hand it to an incoming management team without losing a step. They were buying IP embedded in process documentation, training programs, technology infrastructure, and organizational design. They were buying businesses that run without the founder.

And for that - for a truly systemized, transferable business - they paid 1 to 2 full turns of EBITDA above what domestic PE was offering. On a $3 million EBITDA business, that is $3 million to $6 million more in your pocket at closing.

1-2x EBITDA premium. On a $3M EBITDA business, that is $3M to $6M more at closing - from the same underlying earnings, to a buyer who can actually use what you built.

Why Domestic PE Cannot Compete on Price

To understand why foreign buyers pay more, you first need to understand why domestic PE pays what it pays - and what is structurally constraining their bids.

Private equity firms are financial buyers. They use leverage to acquire businesses, improve operations over 4 to 7 years, and then sell for a higher multiple than they paid. Their return depends on the spread between what they buy at and what they sell at - plus the cash flow generated in between.

That model creates a ceiling on how much PE can pay. If they overpay on entry, the leveraged structure becomes strained. The return math breaks. Funds have LPs - pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds - who demand a specific IRR. Pay too much for the business and you cannot deliver that IRR. Period.

Foreign strategic buyers do not operate under these constraints. A Japanese conglomerate acquiring a US plumbing or HVAC or electrical company is not running a leveraged buyout. They are making a strategic investment with their own balance sheet. They are not trying to sell the business in 5 years. They are trying to own it for 20 or 30 years as a stable, USD-denominated cash flow asset while also using it as a learning laboratory for their domestic operations.

The strategic premium they pay is not generosity. It is math. The value they extract from the acquisition - stable cash flow, US market access, operational knowledge transfer, diversification away from Japan's aging domestic economy - simply exceeds what the numbers on a spreadsheet say the business is worth in isolation. So they bid higher. And they win deals that PE never sees coming.

The $10 Trillion Wave and Why Your Timing Is Right

Here is the context that makes this moment historically significant for any service business owner considering an exit.

10,000 baby boomers turn 65 every single day in America. That number will continue for the better part of the next decade. These are not people approaching retirement in some abstract future sense. These are business owners - people who built service companies, trade businesses, professional firms - who are right now, actively, beginning to think about what comes next.

The aggregate value of the businesses they own is staggering. Approximately $10 trillion in business wealth is changing hands over this generational transfer period. $10 trillion. That is not a rounding error. That is the largest transfer of privately held business wealth in American history.

And here is the problem: 78% of these business owners have no succession plan. None. Not a partial plan. Not an outline. Nothing documented. The business - the thing they built over 20 or 30 years - has no path forward that does not depend entirely on the founder staying in the seat.

For foreign strategic buyers watching this landscape, this represents exactly the type of market dislocation that creates premium acquisition opportunities. There are tens of thousands of valuable, cash-generating service businesses about to come to market - many of which will be rushed, undervalued, or sold to the first buyer who shows up with a check. The businesses that are the exceptions - the ones that are systemized, documented, and demonstrably transferable - stand out like beacons.

$10 trillion in business wealth changing hands. 78% of those owners have no succession plan. Foreign buyers are watching - and they pay premiums for the rare businesses that are actually ready to transfer.

What "Systemized" Actually Means to a Foreign Buyer

Foreign buyers - particularly those operating from a different country, culture, and language - have an even more acute sensitivity to owner dependency than domestic buyers. A domestic PE firm can put their own operating partner in the business. They can hire from the local talent pool. They understand the regulatory environment and the customer psychology.

A Japanese conglomerate cannot do any of that easily. They need the business to run. They need the systems to be documented so clearly that a new management team - potentially one trained in Japan on American business practices - can pick up the playbook and execute it. They need the processes to be transferable across cultures, not locked in the head of an owner who has been doing it instinctively for 30 years.

This is why they pay more for businesses that have built this infrastructure. And this is why the 78% of business owners with no succession plan - no documented systems, no management depth, no operational independence from the founder - are essentially invisible to the best-paying buyers in the market.

Systemized, in this context, means five specific things:

Documented processes. Every repeatable function in the business has a written standard operating procedure. Sales, service delivery, customer onboarding, quality control, HR, finance. Not in the owner's head. On paper. In a format that a capable person could follow without asking the founder how it works.

A management team that operates independently. The business has an operator - a GM or operations manager - who makes day-to-day decisions without the owner's input. The owner has moved out of the operational seat and into the strategic seat. A foreign buyer needs to know that removing the founder does not collapse the organization.

Predictable, recurring revenue. Service agreements, maintenance contracts, retainer relationships - revenue that does not require re-selling every month. Foreign buyers value predictability above almost everything else. Recurring revenue signals that the customer relationship belongs to the business, not the founder.

Diversified customer base. No single customer represents more than 10% of revenue. No single service line represents more than 40%. Concentration is risk, and risk suppresses price. A diversified customer base signals institutional resilience.

Clean, auditable financials. GAAP-compliant statements. Personal expenses separated from business expenses. Tax returns that match the P&L. Foreign buyers often engage international accounting firms to conduct due diligence, and any ambiguity in the financials kills the deal faster than almost anything else.

The Pride You Have Earned - and the Price You Deserve

There is something important that does not get said enough in conversations about business exits. The business you built is not just a financial asset. It is a life's work. It represents thousands of early mornings, difficult decisions, payrolls met during lean months, customers who trusted you, employees who built their careers under your roof.

You did not build it to be carved up for parts. You did not build it to be handed over at a discount because the local PE firm is running a leveraged model with a tight IRR constraint. You built something worth owning. Something worth preserving. Something worth paying a premium for.

Foreign strategic buyers - particularly those from business cultures where long-term ownership is the norm rather than the exception - often understand this better than domestic financial buyers. They are not looking to flip your business in 5 years. They are looking to operate it, grow it, and learn from it for decades. They want to buy something good and keep it good. That alignment of values - between what you built and what they want to own - is part of why the transaction feels different, and why the price reflects it.

But here is the hard truth: they will only pay the premium for a business that actually works without you. If you are the business - if every important decision runs through you, if your customers call your personal cell, if your team cannot function when you take a week off - then there is nothing to transfer. The premium evaporates. The business is only worth what a domestic buyer can rebuild it into, which is a fraction of what it is worth as a going concern.

10,000 boomers turn 65 daily. The owners who built transferable businesses will attract a global pool of buyers. Those who didn't will compete on price with whoever is left.

Why Most Owners Never See This Buyer Pool

If foreign strategic buyers pay more, why do most service business exits not involve them?

Three reasons. And all three are fixable.

Most brokers do not know how to reach them. The typical business broker works a regional buyer list: local PE firms, strategic acquirers in adjacent industries, owner-operators looking to buy their first business. They do not have relationships with the international business development offices of Japanese trading companies or the M&A teams of European conglomerates. Running a process that reaches foreign buyers requires a different kind of advisor with a different kind of network.

Most businesses are not positioned for foreign buyers. The documentation, management infrastructure, and operational independence that foreign buyers require does not happen by accident. It takes 12 to 24 months of deliberate preparation. Most owners list their business before doing this work, which means they are never competitive for the buyers who pay the most.

Most owners do not know the option exists. This is the simplest problem - and the most common. The overwhelming majority of service business owners, when they start thinking about an exit, assume the universe of buyers is domestic: PE firms, strategic competitors, or owner-operators. The idea that a Japanese homebuilder or a German industrial conglomerate might want to own their HVAC company or their commercial cleaning operation simply does not occur to them.

Awareness is the first step. But awareness without preparation is worthless. The businesses that successfully sell to foreign buyers at premium prices are the ones that spent 12 to 24 months building the transferable infrastructure those buyers demand - before going to market.

How to Position Your Business for a Foreign Buyer Premium

The preparation required to attract foreign strategic buyers is not different from the preparation required to maximize any exit. It is the same foundation, executed at a higher standard, with more attention to documentation and transferability.

Start with an honest assessment of owner dependency. How many decisions per day require your input? How many customer relationships exist because of your personal connection? How many processes exist only in your memory? The honest answers to these questions define your starting point. Everything else is about closing those gaps.

Build the management layer. This is the single highest-leverage investment you can make before an exit. Hire or promote an operator who can run the business day-to-day. Give them the authority and the information to make decisions independently. Spend 12 months moving yourself out of operations and into strategy. When a foreign buyer comes to evaluate the business, they need to see an organization, not an individual.

Document everything. This is not optional for foreign buyers. It is the price of entry. Start with your highest-volume, highest-risk processes. Service delivery. Customer onboarding. Pricing methodology. Quality control. Safety protocols. Every SOP you write is a direct contribution to your exit valuation.

Build recurring revenue. Maintenance agreements, service contracts, retainer relationships - any structure that creates predictable, repeating cash flow. Recurring revenue is not just a valuation driver. It is a proof point that the customer relationship belongs to the business, not the founder. Foreign buyers will pay a meaningful premium for a company with 30% or more recurring revenue versus one with near zero.

Work with an advisor who knows this buyer pool. The difference between a broker who pitches your business to domestic PE and an advisor who runs a structured process reaching foreign strategic buyers is potentially millions of dollars at your closing table. This is not the place to optimize for the cheapest representation.

The Businesses That Get Bought on Their Own Terms

The $10 trillion transfer is already happening. 10,000 boomers turning 65 every day are not all going to exit at the same moment - but the wave is real, it is large, and it is ongoing. The supply of businesses coming to market over the next decade will be significant.

Not all of them will get the same outcome. The businesses that built transferable systems - that operate without their founders, that have documented processes, that generate predictable recurring revenue - will attract the full spectrum of buyers, including foreign strategic acquirers willing to pay premiums that domestic PE cannot match. These owners will exit on their terms, at prices that reflect what they actually built.

The 78% who have no succession plan, no management depth, and no documented systems will be competing for the buyers who are left after the premium buyers have made their choices. That is a different market, at a different price, with different terms.

You built something real. Something that has generated wealth, employed people, and served a community for decades. The question is not whether your business has value. The question is whether that value is accessible to the buyers who will pay the most for it - or locked inside your head in a way that only the cheapest buyers can extract.

The Japanese homebuilder trend is not an anomaly. It is a signal. Foreign capital has identified what good American service businesses are worth. The only remaining question is whether you will position yours to capture that premium before the wave passes.

You earned this. Let's make sure you get it.
