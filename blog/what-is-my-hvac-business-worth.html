What Is My HVAC Business Worth? How Buyers Calculate Value and What You Can Do About It

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
March 5, 2026 • 8 min read

One of the most consistent patterns in the trades: HVAC owners with $10M-$20M in revenue who have assumed a retirement number for years without ever testing it against actual valuation math. The gap between assumption and reality is frequently $2M to $4M - not because the business is not good, but because the revenue mix does not support the multiple the owner is expecting.

A $15M HVAC business where 80% of revenue is single-project residential installation and the owner handles key customer relationships personally will have adjusted EBITDA in the $1.3M-$1.6M range and command a 3.5x to 4.0x multiple from most buyers. That math produces $4.5M to $6.4M. If the owner has been mentally planning on $8M, the gap is real - and it closes with operational work, not negotiation.

The gap between what HVAC owners think their business is worth and what buyers will actually pay is one of the most consistent patterns in the trades. Here's how to close it.

How HVAC Business Valuations Actually Work

HVAC businesses are valued almost universally on a multiple of EBITDA - Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization - with adjustments. The adjustments matter as much as the EBITDA itself.

Adjusted EBITDA starts with your reported operating income and adds back:

  • Owner compensation above a market replacement salary (typically $80,000 to $120,000 for a GM role)
  • Personal expenses run through the business
  • One-time expenses that won't recur (lawsuit settlements, equipment write-offs, etc.)
  • Non-cash expenses like depreciation that don't reflect cash generation

Once you have your adjusted EBITDA, the multiple applied depends on:

  • Business size (larger businesses get higher multiples)
  • Revenue quality (recurring service vs. project-based)
  • Buyer type (individual vs. strategic vs. PE platform)
  • Operational independence from the owner
  • Customer concentration and contract structure
  • Geographic market and growth trajectory

Current HVAC Multiple Ranges (2026)

The HVAC industry has been one of the most actively acquired trade verticals for the past five years. PE-backed platforms have been aggressive. Here's what the multiples look like by business profile:

Small HVAC business - $2M to $5M revenue, owner-operated, primarily residential installation: 2.5x to 3.5x EBITDA. These deals typically involve individual buyers or small regional operators. SBA financing is common. Cash at close is often 60 to 75% of the total.

Mid-size HVAC - $5M to $15M revenue, mixed residential and commercial, some service contracts: 3.5x to 5.0x EBITDA. This is where the buyer profile starts to broaden. Regional HVAC groups, strategic acquirers, and smaller PE platforms all participate. The multiple spread is wide because business quality at this level varies significantly.

Larger HVAC platform - $15M+ revenue, significant service contract base, professional management team: 5.0x to 7.0x EBITDA. These deals attract PE platforms looking to make a platform acquisition they can build on. The premium is real - and it's specifically driven by recurring revenue and operational infrastructure.

A $15M HVAC business with $1.8M adjusted EBITDA and 30% recurring service revenue might realistically transact at 5.5x - a $9.9M deal. The same revenue with 5% recurring revenue and an owner-led operation might sell at 3.8x - $6.8M. That's a $3.1M difference on identical revenue for two businesses of the same size. The difference is in the quality and structure of the earnings, not the topline number.

What Drives HVAC Valuations Up

If you're asking what is my HVAC business worth with an eye toward maximizing it, the variables below are where to focus. These are the specific characteristics PE buyers and strategic acquirers pay premiums for.

Service agreements and maintenance contracts. Annual maintenance agreements with recurring monthly or quarterly billing are the most valuable revenue in an HVAC business. They're predictable, sticky, and create a baseline that reduces revenue volatility. A business with 500 active service agreements looks meaningfully different in a buyer's model than one that has no service contract program.

A real operations layer. An HVAC business where the owner handles service escalations, key customer relationships, and final bids is an owner-dependent business - and it gets discounted accordingly. A business with a service manager, a sales lead, and a dispatcher who don't require the owner to function adds real valuation points. This is the single most actionable area for most HVAC owners. More detail in our article on owner-dependent business valuation.

Commercial relationships with contracts. Ongoing commercial maintenance relationships - office buildings, retail, light industrial - with documented service agreements are viewed as lower-risk revenue than residential replacement cycles. Buyers can underwrite them more precisely.

Clean licensing and regulatory compliance. HVAC licensing is state and often locality-specific. A business that has properly licensed technicians, clean EPA 608 certification records, and documented compliance history is lower-risk than one that has cut corners operationally. Buyers discover this in due diligence. Issues slow deals or kill them.

What Compresses HVAC Valuations

Installation-heavy revenue with no service tail. New construction and equipment replacement jobs are one-time. Buyers can't count on them repeating. If 85% of your revenue is installation and 15% is service, that's a fundamentally different business risk profile than the reverse.

Customer concentration. One large commercial property management company representing 35% of your revenue is a single risk point that sophisticated buyers price into every model. Diversification - or at minimum, documented long-term contract structure with that key customer - is essential.

Aging or untracked fleet and equipment. HVAC businesses with aging truck fleets, mixed equipment ages, and no clear capex schedule give buyers a cost hangover to price. They'll estimate replacement costs and deduct them from the offer. A clean fleet picture with recent investment is a simpler story to tell.

Recurring service revenue isn't just valuable - it's the difference between a 3.5x business and a 5.5x business.

Getting to Your Real Number

The answer to "what is my HVAC business worth" isn't a formula. It's a conversation that requires understanding your specific financials, operations, buyer market, and timing.

That said, here's a practical way to start building your own estimate:

  1. Take your most recent three-year average net income
  2. Add back your owner salary above $100,000 (the market replacement cost for your role)
  3. Add back personal expenses, one-time costs, and non-cash charges
  4. That's your adjusted EBITDA
  5. Apply a multiple based on your honest assessment of business quality: 3.0x to 3.5x if owner-dependent and installation-heavy; 4.5x to 5.5x if you have recurring revenue, a management team, and clean financials

In our experience, HVAC owners who commit to an 18-24 month preparation process - building a service agreement program, installing a service manager, and improving the revenue mix - consistently see adjusted EBITDA grow 20-30% during that period. The multiple expands at the same time because the revenue quality improves. The combination means the final transaction number is often meaningfully closer to what the owner had in their head all along. The work is real. So are the results.

The number in your head is achievable. It just usually requires work that needs to start earlier than most owners think.
