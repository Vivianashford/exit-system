Succession Planning for Trade Businesses: Three Paths Forward and How to Choose

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
February 22, 2026 • 8 min read

According to the Exit Planning Institute, 73% of business owners have no written succession plan. In the trades, the situation is often worse - most owners have an assumption in their head about what will happen, not a documented plan. That assumption is most commonly "my kid will take it over" or "my best manager will buy me out." Both paths can work. Neither works without deliberate planning and an honest conversation that many owners never have.

The pattern we consistently see: a founder approaches 60, the business is strong, and the succession path they assumed was in place - family or management - has never been confirmed, funded, or structured. A health event, a burnout episode, or an unsolicited offer from a PE platform forces the decision. And because the business was informally optimized for one transition path that never materializes, the founder enters a third-party sale process without the preparation that would have commanded a premium.

Succession planning for trade businesses starts with one honest question: who is actually taking this over, and do they want to?

Path One: Family Transition

Transferring a trade business to a family member - a child, a spouse, a sibling - is the most emotionally satisfying path and often the most financially complicated one.

The core challenge is that family transitions require the next generation to fund the purchase in some form - and most family members don't have $3M to $8M sitting around. This usually means some combination of:

  • Seller financing - the founder effectively loans the business to the successor, who pays out of future profits over 5 to 10 years
  • Gifting - partial transfer of ownership through annual gift tax exclusions over time, reducing the purchase price
  • ESOP or internal structure - for larger businesses, an Employee Stock Ownership Plan can provide tax advantages and a structured transfer mechanism
  • SBA financing - for successors who qualify, SBA loans can fund a significant portion of the purchase price

The honest math on family succession is that it typically produces less immediate cash for the founder than a third-party sale. You are, in effect, trading maximum liquidity for legacy, reduced taxes, and control over what happens to the business after you leave.

This is a legitimate trade-off - but it needs to be made with eyes open. Too many founders discover the liquidity gap mid-transfer, after they've already committed to a path, and end up underfunded in retirement as a result.

Family succession works best when:

  • The successor genuinely wants to run the business (not just reluctantly take it on)
  • The successor has the capability to lead, not just operate
  • The founder can live comfortably on less than full market-rate proceeds
  • The founder is prepared to step fully back and let the successor lead

Path Two: Management Buyout

A management buyout - where a key employee or leadership team purchases the business from the founder - is the path that often gets overlooked. It's more viable than most founders assume, and it can be structured in ways that protect both sides.

In a typical management buyout (MBO) for a trade business, the deal structure involves:

  • A down payment from the management team (often 10 to 20% of purchase price, sourced from their own capital or SBA financing)
  • Seller financing for the balance, paid from business profits over 5 to 10 years
  • A clean transition plan with defined handoff milestones

The advantage of an MBO is operational continuity. The buyers know the business. Customers know them. Field teams know them. There's no integration risk, no culture disruption, and no anxiety about what happens to the people you've worked with for 20 years.

The risk is financial: you are carrying a note on the sale. If the business declines under new management, your repayment is at risk. This is why MBO structures typically include strong operational safeguards, reporting requirements, and sometimes retained minority interests for the founder during a transition period.

For succession planning for trade businesses, an MBO is often the highest-trust path when you have a capable operator in place who has already been running daily operations. The business has, in effect, already been showing the buyers what it looks like without you.

Path Three: Third-Party Sale

A third-party sale - to a strategic acquirer, PE platform, or individual buyer - typically produces the highest immediate cash value and is the cleanest exit for a founder who wants full liquidity and a defined end date.

For most trade business owners over 58, this is the path that best serves retirement and financial independence. The market for trade businesses is active, PE-backed platforms are acquiring aggressively, and multiples remain strong for businesses with clean operations and professional management.

The trade-off with a third-party sale is control over what happens next. Once you close, the business belongs to someone else. Employees may see changes in culture, process, or benefits. Your name may remain on the trucks or eventually fade. For founders who are deeply attached to the legacy of what they built, this can be emotionally harder than the transaction itself suggests.

Done well, a third-party sale produces the capital for the founder to create a lasting legacy on their own terms - philanthropy, family trusts, a new venture. The business was the vehicle. The sale is not the end of the story.

Succession isn't about who takes your seat. It's about whether the business continues to serve the people who depend on it - including you.

How to Choose Your Path

The right succession path depends on four factors: your financial needs, your emotional priorities, the capability of the people around you, and the state of the business itself.

Answer these questions honestly before you commit to a path:

  1. What do you need from the sale to fund your retirement comfortably, with no financial stress?
  2. Is there a specific person - family or management - who wants to run this business and can demonstrate they're capable?
  3. How much do you care about what the business looks like after you leave?
  4. Are you prepared to carry seller financing risk if a family member or manager doesn't perform?
  5. What does your financial advisor say you need from a liquidity event to meet your retirement goals?

Most trade business owners who work through this honestly end up with a clearer answer than they expected. The emotional pull toward family succession is real - but it needs to be tested against the practical realities of liquidity and successor capability.

What All Three Paths Have in Common

Regardless of which succession path you choose, the preparation is largely the same: clean financials, reduced owner dependency, documented operations, and a business that can be owned and run by someone other than you.

A family member taking over needs the same infrastructure a PE buyer needs. An MBO buyer needs the same clean books a strategic acquirer needs. The work of building a transferable business is the work of succession - regardless of who ends up owning it.

If you haven't started that work, start now. The founders who wait until they've decided on a path - and then try to prepare - are the ones who either rush the transition or accept less than the business is worth.
