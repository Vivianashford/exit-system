What Buyers Look for When Buying a Business: Inside the Due Diligence Checklist

JH
Jean Louis Hardy The Arena Partners
January 25, 2026 • 8 min read

Most deal re-trades and price reductions happen because of something the seller knew about but did not think a buyer would care about. Customer concentration is the most common trigger - a single client representing 30-40% of revenue, a contract up for renewal with no signed extension, a key relationship tied personally to the founder. These are not hidden problems. They are things the seller considered non-issues. Buyers see them as concentrated risk and price accordingly.

According to BizBuySell transaction data, deal re-trades occur in a significant percentage of transactions above $2M, with the most frequent triggers being financial discrepancies and undisclosed risk factors discovered in due diligence. A seller who understands what buyers look for before they are already in a process avoids this. A seller who learns it during due diligence absorbs the cost - often $500,000 to $1M or more in price reduction or deal structure changes.

The Pre-LOI Evaluation: What Buyers Are Thinking Before They Make an Offer

Before a buyer submits a Letter of Intent, they've already done significant evaluation. The information in your Confidential Information Memorandum (the document your advisor prepares to market the business) forms their initial picture, and the questions they ask in early conversations reveal what they're probing for.

The initial evaluation focuses on four things:

Revenue quality and predictability. Is this revenue we can rely on after we close? Project-based revenue that varies significantly year to year raises questions. Recurring revenue tied to contracts or service agreements is valued more confidently. Buyers want to see a revenue story that makes sense - not just a number.

EBITDA margin and trajectory. Is the business getting more or less profitable over time? A business with declining margins - even if revenue is growing - signals either pricing problems or cost structure issues that a buyer will need to fix at their own expense. Improving margins are a positive signal. Flat is acceptable. Declining is a negotiating advantage for the buyer.

Customer and revenue concentration. How fragile is the revenue base? One large customer representing a significant share of revenue is the most common concern. Geographic concentration - a service business heavily dependent on a single market that's showing softness - is another. Buyers price concentration risk directly into their offers.

Owner dependency. The question that shapes the entire offer. A business that needs the current owner to keep running is fundamentally higher risk than one that operates independently. This single factor influences both the price and the deal structure more than almost any other variable. The full picture is in our article on owner-dependent business valuation.

What Buyers Look for in Due Diligence: The Financial Track

Once an LOI is signed and formal due diligence begins, buyers deploy accountants and financial analysts to verify everything the seller represented. The financial due diligence track is the most structured and document-intensive part of the process.

The specific items requested in financial diligence for a typical trade business:

  • Three years of tax returns (federal and state)
  • Three years of monthly P&L statements
  • Three years of balance sheets
  • Current accounts receivable aging report
  • Current accounts payable aging report
  • Trailing 12 months of revenue by customer
  • Payroll records and compensation summary for all employees
  • Owner compensation detail and any family member compensation
  • Capital expenditure history for 3 years
  • All outstanding loans, lines of credit, and equipment financing
  • Bank statements for the past 12 to 24 months

Buyers are looking for three things in this data: confirmation that the numbers you represented are real, identification of add-backs that are legitimate and defensible, and discovery of any surprises - liabilities, revenue declines, unusual expenses - that weren't disclosed upfront.

Surprises in due diligence are the enemy of a smooth close. Whether the issue is a revenue decline in the most recent quarter, an unexpected liability, or inconsistencies between the CIM representations and the underlying financials, surprises create leverage for buyers to either reduce the price or exit the deal entirely.

Sellers who have done their own internal diligence review before going to market - identifying and addressing issues before buyers find them - move through this phase significantly faster and with fewer price adjustments.

What Buyers Look for in Due Diligence: The Operational Track

Parallel to the financial review, buyers evaluate operations. The goal is to understand how the business actually works and what the post-close integration looks like.

Operational due diligence typically covers:

Key employee interviews. Buyers want to meet the management team, the service manager, the office lead - whoever runs the business day-to-day below the owner level. They're evaluating competence, retention risk, and cultural fit. Employees who seem uncertain about the future of the business or their own role post-close raise red flags.

Customer reference calls. For meaningful customer accounts, buyers often request permission to speak directly with the customer. They're looking for validation that the customer relationship is real, transferable, and not personally tied to the seller. Customer references and financial review tend to surface the same issues - revenue concentration, contract renewal uncertainty, and key relationships tied to the founder rather than the organization. Buyers who find the same problem through two independent channels have significant negotiating leverage.

Licensing and compliance review. All required business licenses, contractor licenses, specialty certifications, and regulatory compliance documentation are reviewed. Any gaps - expired licenses, uncertified employees in roles requiring certification - create liability that buyers want addressed before close or price-adjusted for.

Equipment and asset condition review. For trade businesses, the physical condition and age of the truck fleet, tools, and equipment is evaluated directly. Buyers model out replacement capex based on equipment age. Old, unmaintained equipment is a cost they'll carry post-close - and they price that into their offer.

Contract review. Every material customer contract, vendor agreement, subcontractor relationship, and lease is reviewed. Buyers are looking for change-of-control provisions (contracts that terminate or require consent upon a business sale), assignment restrictions, and any non-standard terms that affect the value they're acquiring.

Every surprise a buyer finds in due diligence is either a price reduction or a deal killer. Find them yourself first.

The Management Presentation: What Buyers Are Watching For

Most sale processes include a management presentation - a formal meeting where the buyer's team sits down with the seller and, ideally, the seller's key managers. This is not just an information exchange. It's an evaluation.

Buyers are assessing:

  • The depth and quality of the management team below the owner
  • Whether the owner's narrative about the business matches the financial data
  • The owner's emotional readiness to exit (burned-out sellers who are desperate to leave are a negotiating signal)
  • Whether the key employees seem like people they want to work with post-close
  • The operational maturity of the business - does it feel like a system, or does it feel like it runs through the owner's personal effort?

The most effective management presentations are those where the key managers speak confidently about their domains - where the service manager can explain the dispatch system, where the office manager can walk through the billing process - without the owner needing to explain everything. That's the presentation of a business that can be owned post-close.

What Creates Confidence and What Creates Doubt

After working through the due diligence process from the seller's side many times, the patterns are clear. Here's what creates confidence in a buyer and what creates doubt.

Confidence builders:

  • Organized data room with all documents pre-populated before requests come in
  • Clear, documented add-back schedule with supporting evidence
  • Key employees who are engaged, articulate, and seem invested in the business's future
  • Customer relationships that are clearly managed through the organization, not just the founder
  • Consistent financials that tell a coherent story year over year
  • A seller who is transparent about challenges and has clear answers for them

Doubt creators:

  • Slow or disorganized document production (signals either disorganization or things to hide)
  • Inconsistencies between verbal representations and written financials
  • Key employees who seem surprised by the sale or anxious about their futures
  • Undisclosed legal matters, pending customer disputes, or recent large customer losses
  • Revenue spike in the most recent year with no clear explanation
  • An owner who struggles to explain how the business would run without them

Understanding what buyers look for when buying a business is most useful when it informs your preparation - not when you're already in the process trying to manage the narrative. The sellers who read this and start working on their data room, their key employee relationships, and their operational documentation 18 months before going to market are the ones who move through diligence smoothly and close at the price they expected.
