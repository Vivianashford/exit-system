Chapter 1: The Valuation Gap Nobody Talks About

Every service business owner has a number in their head - what they think their business is worth. In our experience, that number is wrong 80% of the time. Not by a little. By 30-40%.

The reason is simple: owners value their business based on what they put in. Buyers value it based on what they can take out.

The difference between a 3x and 8x EBITDA multiple has almost nothing to do with revenue. It has everything to do with transferability.

Chapter 2: The 6 Deal-Killers

After working with dozens of exits, these are the six things that kill deals most often:

1. Owner Dependency. If you take 90 days off and the business suffers, buyers see a job, not a company. Every process in your head is a liability on the due diligence checklist.

2. Customer Concentration. If one client is 20%+ of revenue, that is risk the buyer must price in. We have seen deals collapse over this single issue.

3. Declining EBITDA. Revenue might be stable, but margins shrinking means buyers see a downward trajectory. They model forward, not backward.

4. No Documented Processes. Pete Schopen documented every process at his pest control company over 17 years. When Rollins (Orkin's parent) acquired it, they paid a premium. Most businesses have zero documentation.

5. Commingled Finances. Personal expenses running through the business destroy trust in due diligence. Car payments, vacations, family on payroll who don't work.

6. No Succession Plan. No management team. No training systems. One health emergency away from collapse.

Chapter 3: The 2-3 Year Preparation Roadmap

Year 1: Foundation (Months 1-12)

Months 1-3: Document your top 20 processes. Start with the ones that generate the most revenue and the ones that would cause the most damage if done wrong.

Months 4-6: Begin developing your management team. Identify 2-3 key people who can own decisions, not just follow orders. Start delegating real authority.

Months 7-9: Clean up financials. Separate personal from business completely. Work with your CPA to recast historical financials showing true EBITDA.

Months 10-12: Start converting project customers to recurring service agreements. Even a 10% shift moves the needle on valuation.

Year 2: Optimization (Months 13-24)

Months 13-15: Take a 2-week vacation. This is your first real test. What breaks? Fix those systems.

Months 16-18: Reduce customer concentration. Actively pursue new clients in different sectors. No single customer over 15%.

Months 19-21: Build training systems so new hires get productive in weeks, not months. Document onboarding for every role.

Months 22-24: Take a 30-day vacation. If the business runs smoothly, you are getting close.

Year 3: Positioning (Months 25-36)

Months 25-27: Get a formal valuation from an M&A advisor (not a business broker). Understand exactly where you stand and what final adjustments will maximize value.

Months 28-30: Engage your advisory team: M&A advisor, tax attorney, wealth manager. The deal structure matters as much as the headline price.

Months 31-36: Go to market. Your advisor handles confidential outreach, buyer qualification, and negotiation. You keep running the business at peak performance.

Chapter 4: Understanding Your Buyer

For service businesses doing $5M-$50M, there are three types of buyers:

Strategic Buyers - Competitors or companies in adjacent trades who want your customer base, territory, or team. They often pay the highest multiples because of synergies.

Private Equity Platforms - PE firms building roll-up platforms (like Wrench Group, which built a $14 billion home services platform). They are looking for add-on acquisitions with strong operations. They evaluate 3,000+ targets per year.

Individual Buyers - Entrepreneurs or executives who want to own and operate a business. They typically pay lower multiples and may need seller financing.

Key insight: PE platforms and strategic buyers pay 30-50% more than individual buyers for the same business. Your preparation determines which buyer pool you attract.

Chapter 5: The Real Stories

Carl Swann and Swan Energy: Carl built Swan Energy from a small coal operation into a company that did business with John Wayne. His plane flew Ronald Reagan. He was the second-largest shareholder in Penn Square Bank. When it came time to transition, the business had structure. Brandon, who took over, kept the Swan name. He paid Carl's wife $2,500 a month until she passed. Fifteen to twenty family members reached out wanting to be part of the legacy. That is what a transferable business looks like.

Serge the Arborist: My father built an arborist business from nothing over 30 years. Clients loved him. His team respected him. He never documented a single process. When he could no longer work, the business died with him. Thirty years of knowledge, relationships, and reputation - gone.

Pete Schopen: Started Schopen Pest Solutions in a garage. Spent 17 years documenting every process - routes, treatments, customer protocols, safety procedures. When Rollins Inc (Orkin's parent) acquired it for $4 million, the documentation was a major factor in the premium. The business ran without Pete.
